Pathway (summary)

I am a former punk rock drummer turned brain cancer patient turned research dissemination strategist for an academic medical center.

I am fascinated by the impact of transparency on patient-clinician communication (“open notes”), and am on the path to becoming a researcher focused on patient perspectives and stakeholder engagement in research.

Why

It started as a dare.

A national organization was offering large chunks of money (aka grants) to researchers who promised to involve patient stakeholders in the design of said research. I signed up to be a "patient reviewer" for this granting organization and began noticing not many funded projects involved patient stakeholders in a way that I found meaningful. It appeared as though researchers were only including the patients as lip service. For example, one of the studies I reviewed said researchers involved patient stakeholders in the design of the study logo.

I began wondering how the organization would respond if a patient proposed a project without it being tied to an established institution (e.g., academic center, non-profit). Soon after these thoughts I met an amazing researcher at a conference who lost her mother to brain cancer, we developed a proposal to study something that was meaningful to both of us, and submitted it together (with me as the lead).

When we were funded I almost fell over. Everything in me screamed, "You are not qualified to do this! Turn around! Go home!" Friends told me I was having imposter syndrome. I countered, "I am an imposter because I am not a researcher."

What

I co-created the Brain Cancer Quality of Life Collaborative, a multi-stakeholder group dedicated to advancing science and improving the quality of life for people with malignant brain tumors. The group was established in 2017 with funding from PCORI, with a mission to meaningfully engage diverse stakeholders (including patients and care partners) in the prioritization, design, and conduct of research import to people with brain cancer. My co-lead and partner in crime is Bethany Kwan, PhD, at the University of Colorado.

Today, I am also part of the OpenNotes research team at Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center. I am fortunate to now have a formal research mentorship with Cait DesRoches, DrPH, associate professor of medicine at Harvard Medical School.

How

My background is in communications. I have always been fascinated with transforming complicated concepts into something much easier to understand.

After being diagnosed with a malignant brain tumor I was immediately curious about my diagnosis, treatment, and how the brain works. I blogged about this experience for about 8 years straight--creating an open access, layperson perspective of living with a brain tumor.

Being online made it possible for me to connect to many other people facing health issues. Through online communities and social media I learned about patients who were becoming innovators, creators, and researchers who were redesigning their disease experience. I was inspired by these people. They made me wonder, "What am I capable of?" I wanted to bring this spirit of innovation to the brain tumor community. People facing brain tumors are suffering from major, debilitating side effects and poor prognoses. Gaining insights from people who are so ill can be complicated. I wanted to figure out how brain tumor patients and our care partners can become innovators in our own disease experience.

Lessons learned

  • Involving diverse stakeholders (patients and their care partners) in the design and conduct of research sounds simple... but it is not.

  • Researchers and clinicians are fluent in the language of research and are in positions of power. If you want to create a healthy environment for patients and researchers to work together you MUST flatten that hierarchy right away.

  • As a patient-researcher I am in a position to model ways to flatten that hierarchy so everyone can feel comfortable and participate as equals. Some of the ways I do this is by getting everyone to be on a first name basis, asking people to dress casually (e.g., hoodies are OK!), and simplifying language (at first).

  • When researchers switch to jargon, patient stakeholders tend to stop asking questions in fear they will look stupid. I listen for the jargon and watch the faces of stakeholders, and act as a proxy by asking researchers to reframe in plain language. Over time, the researchers learn to start talking like human beings again, and the stakeholders start becoming fluent in research methods. It is really rad.

  • Interestingly, I have also noticed researchers and clinicians might go silent when working with patient stakeholders. The “woke” researchers want to respect the voice of patients but this can be a detriment when the researcher voice is lost. It is a delicate balance.

Vision

I think the future of patients as research partners is being formed and co-created right now by the people who are opening new pathways in this space and the formally trained clinicians/researchers who are joining us as partners. Patient innovators and researchers must partner with traditional researchers to bridge the gap between our two cultures. What is happening right now is not an us versus them. This is about building honest and respectful partnerships. We can learn a lot from each other.
