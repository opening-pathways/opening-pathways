Pathway (summary)

Fighting for the right of patients to have access to our health data.

Why

I have an implantable cardiac defibrillator. I wanted to raise awareness of the fact that patients with pacemakers and other implantable devices have no authority or sovereignty over the data collected from our cardiac implantable electronic devices.

What

In 2012, I was denied health insurance due to a pre-existing condition. The cutoff of access to my medical care demanded a radical approach. After taking a course on the fundamentals of cardiac rhythm management, I purchased a used pacemaker programmer in order to perform my own pacemaker follow ups. I also started collaborating with other advocates who were working on their own data liberation projects. My hope was to learn from their experience, join forces, and increase our collective effectiveness.

How

I was lucky to meet someone at a Quantified Self meeting who invited me to speak at a TEDx event in Cambridge, MA. The TEDx talk was well received and MIT Technology Review published an article about my efforts. Other media outlets picked up the story and it started to get traction. Eventually, NPR, the Wall Street Journal and — more recently — the Economist, covered the topic.

Lessons learned

Advocacy can be exhausting and frustrating work. Expect to come up against many brick walls and be aware that progress toward a resolution might be slow. But also remember that nothing worth doing is ever easy. Stick to your principles, be patient, and press ahead at your own pace. It is also important to recognize the need to take a break from it all to focus on self-care.

Vision

While a solution to the lack of access has not yet been found, new concerns have emerged regarding the privacy and cybersecurity vulnerabilities of implantable electronic devices. We have much to do before we can declare sovereignty over our health data and full authority over the devices implanted inside our own bodies.
