Provide the Pencils, Don’t Write the Story: Recommendations for Young Researchers

Author: Rebecca Monteleone

Submitted: 2019-09-11T23:04:32.133Z

Reading time: 7 min (as on site)

An hour earlier, I had been giddy on my own arrogant assumption that I was about to offer a platform for people to share their experiences, and now I was confronted with the reality that I didn’t know enough for any of them to want to.

Early in 2019, I posted an interview recruitment ad in a popular social media group for people using DIY closed loop systems for T1D. Within minutes, I received a response. And then another. I was immediately smug, feeling self-satisfied that I, the benevolent researcher, was offering a platform to amplify all these underserved patient voices. I logged in to reap my participants. I began to read.

                  “I think it’s misleading…”

                  “…your terminology is a little off…”

                  “You should probably just ask…”

Rather than discover a crowd of eager and grateful participants, I was confronted with polite but firm resistance: the language I had used in the recruitment ad was not just inaccurate, it was alienating to some members of the DIY community. An hour earlier, I had been giddy on my own arrogant assumption that I was about to offer a platform for people to share their experiences, and now I was confronted with the reality that I didn’t know enough for any of them to want to. I had tripped on my very first step. Rather than be humbled, I was a little irritated that someone in the community had called me out. My ego bruised, I reflected on that irritation and realized it was rooted in a small, not-so-pretty part of me that wanted to be the expert in the room, even when the point of the project I was recruiting for was to highlight the expertise of the DIY community. In the past, I have written and spoken about how patients knowledge and experience get dismissed by researchers and clinicians. And yet here I was, dismissing these comments and missing the irony of it completely. Cowed and with new insight into my own position as a researcher, I edited my recruitment ad to reflect the feedback I’d received. Soon after, I scheduled my first interview.

For the last six months, my job has been to listen.

My dissertation work has since allowed me to interview a number of users, creators, and parents in the DIY community. I entered this project wanting to understand the human side of DIY – how people interact with medical devices, what influences the choices they make, and how they think about themselves, their bodies, and their communities. To do that, I had to learn to listen, not just to the responses to the questions I asked, but to the questions I failed to ask, the pieces I’d overlooked, and the stories I couldn’t have imagined. I also had to learn to listen to the pauses as people reflected on frustrating or troubling experiences and the laughter as they recounted an absurd interactions with their doctors. I had to learn to see the full lives lived by the people who graciously shared their stories. Looping was important to them, sure, but to truly understand their personal experiences, asking about their coding skills simply wasn’t enough.

I’ve learned a lot so far, but mostly I’ve learned how little I know. Below, I’ve drafted a list of recommendations for young researchers engaging with patient communities. These are both lessons I’ve learned and aspirations I work toward. It is a living document that I plan to return to as I continue my dissertation work and beyond.

1.     Be Flexible

As a young, green researcher, it can be easy to want to control every interaction you have with your partners, participants, or interlocutors. We are unsure, lack confidence, and are desperate to prove ourselves. Sometimes this control shows itself in a rigid, inflexible, and unchanging list of interview questions. Sometimes it looks like turning down opportunities or potential research partners that don’t line up exactly with the research plan you made up six months before you’d ever met a single person in the community. I used to think rigidly controlling a conversation, an interview, or a study proved I was a competent, confident researcher. Instead, it did the opposite. By building flexibility into your research agenda, your interview questions, or your timeline, you can allow your patient partners to shape your collaborative project. Ultimately, the research only exists because of your patient partners. You can offer them pencils, but you cannot write their stories for them.

2.     Share Ownership of the Narrative

For most of the history of the social sciences, people participated in interviews, surveys, or experiments, and then never heard from the researcher again. They had no input in what the study was meant to do and had no control over their contribution. Whatever they added was immediately lost in the obscure and obtuse academic world. People felt alienated, exploited, or distanced from research outcomes that filtered their experiences through a researcher’s analytical lens without consultation or comment. Researchers wrote and spoke only to each other, and very little changed in the world around them. To shift this paradigm, we must think about the research process differently. This could mean conducting research on behalf of patient advocacy groups, co-designing projects, or exchanging ideas, comments, and data with a broad group of stakeholders. It might mean returning both raw data (i.e. audio recordings, transcripts, focus group videos, field notes) and write-ups to the people who participated in creating them, and accepting their revisions, comments, and concerns. What it ultimately means is creating partnerships, sharing work, being transparent in your process, and accepting feedback graciously.

3.     Appreciate an N of 1

Individual stories can be as powerful as statistical significance. It can be so tempting to sand off all of the unique edges of people’s stories so they fit into some broader, generalizable narrative. Better yet if it is a theory that will gain you, the researcher, some international fame. When we do this, people stop being people and start to become “objects of research.” Working with the DIY community, it has become glaringly obvious to me that every story is unique. To generalize these stories obscures the pieces of them that make them important. I strongly believe that deeply contextual, personal narratives have an important role to play alongside the massive data sets and impressive p values. Preserve the power of the singularity. 

4.     Be Surprised

Don’t only ask questions you know the answers to. Don’t even assume you know what questions to ask. Allow yourself to be surprised and allow yourself to let your partners or participants know you are surprised. I was recently interviewing a member of the DIY community when they started talking about a new therapy they were using. “Have you heard of X?” they asked. My immediate impulse –as a young researcher eager to prove myself as competent and knowledgeable – was to lie and say “Yes.” I could just Google it later, I told myself.  To do that, however, would shut down that conversational pathway completely. I hadn’t heard of X; what kinds of follow-up questions could I possibly ask? What might I miss by pretending I knew more than the person whose expertise I was seeking in the first place? Not knowing is one of the thrills of research, so let yourself be a student with your patient partners, participants, or interlocutors.

We as research partners enter into this research because we believe there is knowledge and expertise in this community. Sometimes, we need to remind ourselves to be humble enough to discover it.
