My first experiences working with patients as a researcher was, in fact, a false start. As a newly inducted PhD student at the prestigious Institute of Psychiatry I obligingly followed my eminent mentors, read all that I could about my area of forthcoming expertise (ALS / MND / Lou Gehrig’s disease), and happily wore the nice little halo that society gives you for “helping to find a cure for a terrible disease”. Each day I would visit patients and caregivers at home to administer a variety of neuropsychological tests, hoping to unlock an interesting puzzle from an obscure corner of the research field studied only by a dozen or so researchers on the planet in any detail. Shortly after I started the program, my professor asked for a volunteer to take over the running of a small online community for patients living with ALS/MND who were using the web and whatever assistive technology they could muster to talk to one another, share tips on living with the disease, and discuss research. As a card-carrying nerd I volunteered and saved the fledgling community from the scrap heap as the grant which supported it was about to expire and the staff who set it up had now moved on to other things.

Fully expecting a virtual ticker tape parade and a hero’s welcome for taking on this thankless task, I was taken aback when one of the first discussion threads to take part on the forum was areas of research that patients considered a waste of time, effort, and money when resources were in such short supply and attention should only be directed to clinical trials for the cure. Top of the list? My research study. “We’re dying for lack of clinical trials, so why should anyone bother filling out your questionnaires so you can be paid from the money we raise for charity?” This was not how I expected things to go. Worse still, some of my clinical mentors who I knew were internationally renowned thought leaders did not, as it turned out, have universally appreciated bedside manners. While patients and their families that we saw every week in clinic seemed stoic, positive, and universally grateful, the same population given a safe space to vent and the time to painstakingly tap out words with their eye-trackers, finger switches, and trackballs were, well, pissed off. And not just pissed off with their disease, they were pissed off with me, with my colleagues, with the state of the whole field.

There was a decision to be made there – whose side was I on anyway? In my head I’d always seen it as medicine against a disease, using the careful rigor of science to build grains of sands and stand on the shoulders of giants and all that. But the more I tried to explain, liaise, defend, and placate, the more I realized that such a slow and plodding approach was a great fit for my decades-long future career, but maybe not so hot if you’d been given between two and five years to live. I came to realize that the rightful anger of a group of people who’d been patronized, patted on the head, and ignored was not a problem with them, it was a problem with us. And so I decided to forge a new path where I would act as a conduit of the patient voice, not attempt to speak on their behalf.

You can read it in the titles of my papers, which attempt to convey what the patients want not what researchers are interested in studying or had thought to be closed issues:

·      Subjects no more: what happens when trial participants realize they hold the power?

·      ALS patients request more information about cognitive symptoms

·      Reassessing received wisdom in ALS – pain is common when studied systematically

·      Increasing Patient Involvement in Drug Development

·      How Common are ALS Plateaus and Reversals?

·      Patient, study thyself

·      How Engaging Patients Will Change Clinical Trials for the Better

·      Time to deliver patient centered care

·      It’s time to stop saying “the mind is unaffected” in ALS

None of it could have prepared me though for the cruel twist of fate that meant a close family was diagnosed with ALS/MND a few years ago, landing me on the other side of the table as a caregiver. Now I meet colleagues and collaborators from my old life for more prosaic requests: where can we get a wheelchair? Are there any clinical trials ongoing? Can we try this drug for a new symptom? And whilst I would never fully claim to “get it now”, I have seen first-hand that the two or three times a year you’re getting attention from doctors and researchers are the good days. But that’s stacked up against the three hundred and sixty-odd days that you’re not, when one can tire of reading about “breakthroughs” but only in mice or esoteric questionnaire studies that take five years to conclude that having ALS/MND lowers your quality of life.

While the journey I’ve taken to get here is not exactly one I’d recommend – I’ve found a way to channel the dissatisfaction with the status quo into what I hope are effective calls for action to share what I’ve learned with my colleagues.
