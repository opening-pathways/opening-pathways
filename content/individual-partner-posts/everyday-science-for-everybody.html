Everyday science for everybody?

Author: Gary Wolf

Submitted: 2019-09-11T23:02:13.507Z

Reading time: 4 min (as on site)

Can you help? I ask because we have work to do here and we’re waiting for you.

The Opening Pathways project started dramatically. On stage was a person speaking in a tone of maximum pomposity. That was me. I announced that the future would someday arrive in which every person could pull blood glucose data directly from the body, and use this data to control an insulin pump, constituting a functioning artificial pancreas system. Wouldn’t that be wonderful? Now, if we all just closed our eyes and believed…

Wait, there’s an interruption. Dana Lewis had her hand up. “I’m already doing this,” she said.

Oh, Dana, I condescended, I mean more people than just you.

A sea of other hands. We are already doing this. How humiliating for a futurist to find that his sparkly prediction is merely real. I relinquished the stage, and a dozen people who were using the open artificial pancreas system took my place for a discussion about what was happening right now, mostly unseen and unacknowledged by the medical establishment. 

Dana and I had plotted this effect, this artifice, hoping to jar at least somebody in the audience into seeing that it was today’s patient innovators that need to be supported. Actual patients and activists and independent developers of open tools and methods are ready to take their painstakingly handcrafted inventions and extend and improve them until they can benefit everybody. How? Not through making products. Instead, this work is done in the open, and therefore can spread by being adopted and adapted by anybody who understands how to apply it in their own context. These tools are useful to real people today, and because they are actively in use they can be studied by clinical and public health researchers, imitated by device and drug makers, and critically appraised by ethicists, regulators, and community leaders. So, some good news: We don’t have to pour massive sums into the top of a hierarchical system managed by high status rhetoricians who specialized in extracting grants and investments by promising jam tomorrow. Here is some jam today. Anybody hungry?

At first we thought our scheme had worked. Some very sensitive and careful academic researchers proposed collaborations. The key funder of the QS Public Health Symposium, the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, came through with a grant to support Dana, with a little extra for convening and university research. Dana thought, and wrote, and studied, and travelled. She was able to stay in the game. The academic researchers tagged along, and commented, and translated, and - since they are intelligent and productive - may eventually publish things that may eventually be cited to show: health innovation and discovery can be accelerated using peer production and that the world would be a better place if it were easier for everybody to participate.

But here’s the hard question: Who will support this work financially? While commercial enterprises aimed at transforming behavior through collecting and aggregating personal data boast of generating tens and, yes, hundreds of millions of negative dollars, the patients and everyday scientists who are in pursuit of highly consequential questions using empirical methods are going by foot and handcart. There are so many needs, pressing, concrete needs, that it would be irresponsible of me to take this space and not ask for help.

The late Seth Roberts pointed out that clinical and academic researchers have some big advantage over the patients and everyday scientists who are trying to address their own problems in their own communities. Professional scientists often have better equipment and usually have better training. They have financial support and some degree of institutional security. They work in fields that have well established traditions of mentorship and critical feedback. These are big strengths. But patients and everyday scientists also have strengths. One of these strengths is that we care enough to work on the same problems for a long time, because they’re our own problems. A related strength is that we aren’t subject to the professional pressure that crowds academic and clinical researchers into the same narrow subsection of the possibility space. We’re more creative and more open to new ideas. Also, we work inexpensively. OpenAPS and many other communities have learned to survive under financially unfriendly conditions. And, perhaps most importantly, there are a lot more of us. Being stuck is common, but where one person flags or one idea fails, you can find others are nearby.

I’ll end this assessment with a question that is probably just aimed at one single person who may be reading this. (I don’t know who you are, but I hope you will recognize yourself.) Can you help? I ask because we have work to do here and we’re waiting for you.
